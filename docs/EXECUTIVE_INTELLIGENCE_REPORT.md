## 3. FORECAST MODEL ACCURACY ASSESSMENT

> **Methodology:** Walk-forward validation on the last 12 months of actual data.
> Three tiers of accuracy are reported:
> - **Naïve carry-forward** — the simplest possible forecast (baseline floor)
> - **Linear trend extrapolation** — OLS regression on prior 24 months
> - **SARIMAX + XGBoost ensemble** — the production model (5-fold time-series CV on real data)
>
> The ensemble adds seasonal decomposition, 50+ engineered features, and macro regressors.
> MAPE < 5% = stable | 5-15% = moderate | >15% = high-vol (use scenario ranges).

**Reading the ensemble accuracy table:**
- **CV MAPE (5-fold):** The production model's expected forecast error, estimated via walk-forward cross-validation. This is the most realistic accuracy estimate — it tests the model against data it has never seen.
- **CV Dir.Acc ≥ 60%:** Commercially useful — model calls the correct direction (up/down) more often than chance. This is the key metric for hedging trigger timing.
- **SARIMAX AIC:** Model fit quality (lower = better). Used to select ARIMA order. Lower AIC means the model captures more price dynamics with fewer parameters.
- **XGBoost MAPE vs CV MAPE:** When CV MAPE > XGBoost MAPE, the model is overfitting on training data — the CV figure is the honest estimate to use for budgeting.

**Key accuracy findings:**
- **Most predictable:** Copper (CV MAPE 7.0%) — stable enough for annual budget locking
- **Least predictable:** Natural_Gas (CV MAPE 31.1%) — require scenario-based planning rather than point forecasts
- **Best directional accuracy:** Steel (76% correct up/down calls) — most reliable for hedging trigger signals

**What MAPE means in plain language:**

**Directional Accuracy** tells us whether the model correctly predicts the *direction* of price movement (up or down) independently of magnitude. A value above 60% is commercially useful — it means the model is right more often than a coin flip and can support buy/hedge timing decisions.

**Naïve vs Linear model comparison:**
- When the Naïve MAPE is much lower than the Linear MAPE, the commodity is mean-reverting (e.g., Lithium post-2023 correction)
- When the Linear MAPE is lower, there is a clear structural trend — prices are moving consistently in one direction
- Neither benchmark matches the SARIMAX+XGBoost ensemble (which adds macro drivers, seasonality, and regime detection)

### 4.2 Business Implications of Macro Correlations

**What this table tells supply chain and finance teams:**

1. **Oil (WTI) correlation > 0.6 for Polypropylene, ABS Resin, Natural Gas:**  
   These are petrochemical-derived inputs. When oil moves 10%, expect these materials to follow within 1-2 months. An early-warning oil price trigger (e.g., WTI above $90) should automatically flag these material contracts for review.

2. **USD Index (DXY) inverse correlation with Copper, Cobalt, Nickel (-0.4 to -0.6):**  
   LME-traded metals are USD-denominated. When the dollar strengthens, global buyers pay more in local currency terms, reducing demand and suppressing USD prices. For JLR, which reports in GBP, a 5% GBP/USD move creates a double effect — commodity costs change AND FX translation changes.

3. **Manufacturing PMI > 0.4 correlation with Steel, Copper, Nickel:**  
   Global industrial activity is a leading indicator for industrial metals. When PMI dips below 50 (contraction territory), prices typically follow within 1-3 months — offering a hedging window.

4. **CPI rising with most commodities:**  
   This reflects structural inflation embedding itself in input costs. The implication for pricing strategy: if CPI remains elevated above 3%, commodity cost relief is unlikely without a recession.

### 10.2 Forecasting Methodology

The GIC system deploys a **4-method ensemble**:

**Method 1 — SARIMAX (Seasonal ARIMA with exogenous regressors)**
- Captures: autocorrelation in price series, seasonality (12-month cycle), macro factor loadings
- Strengths: interpretable, confidence intervals, handles seasonality well
- Weaknesses: assumes linear relationships, sensitive to structural breaks (e.g. COVID)
- Best for: Natural Gas, Steel (strong seasonal patterns)

**Method 2 — XGBoost (Gradient-boosted ensemble)**
- Features: 50+ engineered features including lags (1/3/6/12m), RSI, MACD, rolling mean/std/min/max, macro regressors (oil, DXY, PMI, CPI, FX), z-scores, calendar encoding
- Strengths: captures non-linear regime shifts, handles macro interactions, robust to outliers
- Cross-validated: TimeSeriesSplit (5 folds) with MAPE and directional accuracy
- Best for: Copper, Lithium, Cobalt, Nickel (macro-driven, non-linear)

**Method 3 — Futures Curve Extraction**
- Uses market-implied forward prices from CME/NYMEX where available
- Zero modelling bias — reflects actual market consensus
- Available for: Copper, Platinum, Palladium, Natural Gas

**Method 4 — Scenario Analysis (Bear/Base/Bull)**
- Expert-driven scenario targets calibrated to macro regime assumptions
- Probability-weighted forecast: 20% Bear / 60% Base / 20% Bull (base case)
- Weights shift dynamically with PMI, DXY, energy prices

### 10.3 Key Limitations

1. **ETF/Equity proxy bias:** Steel (SLX), Aluminum (Alcoa), Cobalt (Glencore), Nickel (Vale) prices are derived from equity/ETF proxies, not direct commodity spot prices. These carry equity risk premium and may diverge from LME physical spot in stress scenarios.

2. **Scale factor sensitivity:** All proxy prices apply static scale factors (e.g., SLX × 7.5). If the equity-commodity relationship changes (e.g., during Alcoa earnings surprises), the commodity price estimate will drift. Monthly recalibration is recommended.

3. **Rhodium/Polypropylene/ABS Resin** are modelled, not market-observed. The O-U process captures mean reversion and volatility clustering but cannot predict sudden structural breaks (e.g., South Africa power outages for Rhodium).

4. **84 monthly observations** (7 years) provides good statistical power for trend/cycle models but may be insufficient to characterise tail-risk scenarios (e.g., 2022-style supercycle recurrence).

## 11. GLOSSARY — KEY TERMS FOR NON-TECHNICAL READERS

| Term | Definition | Corporate Relevance |
|------|-----------|---------------------|
| **MAPE** | Mean Absolute Percentage Error — average % by which forecasts miss actual prices | Lower = more accurate model |
| **RMSE** | Root Mean Squared Error — penalises large errors more than small ones | Relevant for tail-risk sizing |
| **Directional Accuracy** | % of times model correctly predicts up vs. down movement | >60% = useful for trade timing |
| **Annualised Volatility** | Standard deviation of monthly returns × √12 | Drives hedging cost (option premiums) |
| **Z-Score** | Measures how far current price is from its historical mean in standard deviation units | Z>1.5 = 'expensive'; Z<-1.5 = 'cheap' |
| **BOM Weight** | Bill-of-Materials weight — fraction of total raw material cost | Determines COGS sensitivity |
